Oldham Coupling

Parallel Shaft Misalignment & Constant Velocity

What is it?

The Oldham coupling is a mechanism used to transmit torque between two shafts that are parallel but not collinear (lateral misalignment). It consists of three discs: two outer discs connected to the input and output shafts, and a central "floating" disc with tongue-and-groove features on both sides, oriented 90° apart.

Why use it? (The Engineering Advantage)

In precision machinery, aligning two shafts perfectly is often impossible due to manufacturing tolerances or thermal expansion. The Oldham coupling solves this because:

  • High Misalignment Tolerance: It can handle significant parallel offset that would destroy a rigid coupling or cause fatigue in a flexible coupling.
  • Compact Size: It takes up very little axial space compared to a double universal joint setup.
  • Zero Backlash: High-quality versions use a polymer center disc that pre-loads the fit, eliminating play/slop, which is critical for reversing loads.

Engineering Analysis: Homokinetic Motion

The most critical feature of the Oldham coupling is that it is Homokinetic (Constant Velocity).

Unlike a single Universal Joint (Cardan joint), which causes the output speed to fluctuate sinusoidally during rotation (speeding up and slowing down twice per revolution), the Oldham coupling maintains a perfect 1:1 input/output speed ratio at all times.

ω_in = ω_out (Constant)

Design Note: The limit of this mechanism is friction. As the misalignment increases, the sliding velocity of the center disc increases, generating heat and wear. It requires lubrication or self-lubricating materials (like Acetal or Nylon) for the center disc.

Real-World Applications

This mechanism is standard in:

  • 3D Printers & CNC Lead Screws: Connecting the stepper motor to the lead screw. If the motor and screw aren't perfectly aligned, a rigid coupling would cause "Z-wobble" (banding) in the print. The Oldham coupling absorbs the wobble.
  • Servo Mechanisms: Robotics joints where precise angular positioning is required despite slight structural flex.
  • Pumps: Connecting motors to pump heads where seal wear might cause the shaft center to drift over time.
