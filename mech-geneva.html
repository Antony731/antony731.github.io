Geneva Mechanism

Intermittent Motion & Precision Indexing

What is it?

The Geneva drive (or Maltese Cross) is a gear mechanism that translates a continuous rotation into an intermittent rotary motion. The rotating drive wheel has a pin that reaches into a slot of the driven wheel, advancing it by one step. The driven wheel then stays stationary (dwells) while the drive wheel completes its revolution.

Why use it? (The Engineering Advantage)

In automation, we often need a conveyor belt or table to move, stop precisely for an operation (like filling a bottle), and then move again. The Geneva mechanism is perfect for this because:

  • Self-Locking Dwell: During the stationary phase, the convex surface of the input locks against the concave surface of the output. No external brake is needed to hold the load in place.
  • Precision: It provides highly repeatable positioning without complex servo controls.
  • Robustness: It is purely mechanical, making it durable in harsh environments where sensors might fail.

Engineering Analysis: The "Jerk" Limitation

While effective, the Geneva drive has a critical kinematic flaw: Non-zero acceleration at engagement.

When the pin enters the slot, the driven wheel theoretically accelerates from zero instantly. This creates infinite "Jerk" (the rate of change of acceleration), leading to:

  • Shock Loading: High impact stresses on the pin and slot.
  • Vibration: Noise and wear at high speeds.

Engineering Solution: To mitigate this, I designed the entry slot with a curved profile (tangential entry) to smooth out the acceleration curve, although true zero-jerk requires a more complex cam system.

Real-World Applications

You will find this mechanism inside:

  • CNC Machines: Automatic Tool Changers (ATC) often use a Geneva drive to rotate the tool carousel into position.
  • Packaging Industry: Automated assembly lines where products move from station to station.
  • Film Projectors: The classic "click-click" sound of a cinema projector is the Geneva drive pulling the film frame-by-frame.
